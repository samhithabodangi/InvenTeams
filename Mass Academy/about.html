About Us

Kruthi Gundu, Abigail Figueroa, Charuvi Singh, Derek Desrosiers, and Samhitha Bodangi are rising seniors at Mass Academy. Hoping to gain funding and support to develop our invention, we applied and received the Lemelson-MIT InvenTeams Grant, becoming Mass Academy's first InvenTeam. Problem Statement

Visually impaired people (VIPs) encounter significant challenges in accessing written information. Learning Braille from a young age as a VIP has shown to further education and increase employment. However, there is a lack of interactive and affordable educational devices to address the gap in Braille literacy rates for younger VIPs.

Engineering Goal

The goal is to design an portable, yet cost-effective assistive device for VIPs that uses optical character recognition (OCR) to take pictures of text in the environment. The device translates text from the image into Braille configurations and provides a tactile medium for the VIP to read the translated Braille.

The Problem

Much of the information from our environment contains a lot of visual information conveyed through text, such as signage. For the 250 million people who have some form of visual impairment, advertisements, signs, and menus often do not have a Braille translation, which leaves them reliant on audio or sighted individuals to assist them.

Braille Literacy has major implications on future life outcomes for a visually impaired person (VIP), as literacy affects an individual's wellness and socioeconomic status. Despite the well-documented importance of Braille literacy, the Braille literacy of students with visual impairments stands at around 10%. Therefore, the need for affordable Braille educational technology for adolescents is imperative.

Methodology

The development of the tactile Braille display involved a multi-step process. It began with market research and idea formation, where we conducted brainstorming sessions to identify the specific needs of visually impaired people. Initial sketches were created, and we gained feedback from VIPs to prioritize essential features and design elements, ensuring that the device would be both functional and user-friendly.

Once the initial concept was established, computer-aided design (CAD) modeling was employed to visualize and refine the device structure. Various design iterations were made, incorporating community feedback to finalize the deviceâ€™s physical design. The electromechanical construction phase included 3D-printing Braille cells and tools, winding solenoids, soldering PCBs, and assembling the complete device. OCR software was integrated using Microsoft Tesseract software to enable text recognition from camera input. In the end, the software and hardware components were connected to create our first prototype.

How it Works

Our design concept employs electromechanical refreshable Braille modules. The key component of this design is a cam actuator, which consists of an eccentric cam with a magnet embedded in it. This complex is rotated to two stable positions by an electromagnet that changes its polarity. The rotation of the cam causes a Braille dot to be lifted or taken down.

The frame of this device has been designed by the team in SolidWorks and Onshape CAD and 3D printed using an SLA 3D printer. All electrical design was constructed using a PCB board and a Raspberry Pi. Text-to-speech will be implemented using a speech application program interface (SAPI) the given text is converted into audio. Batteries will be added to ensure the device can sustain power whilst being portable.
